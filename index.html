San Francisco is on fire!

The city of San Francisco is the home of a lot of fiery incidents! From 2005 to 2018 there has been recorded more than 45,000 incidents of fire. Fires happen for a lot of different reasons - cooking fires, building fires, vehicle and general outdoor fires are among the first things we think about when thinking about fires. And the people San Francisco are no strangers to these types of fires.

But why does the fires occur? Like any other city; San Francisco has a fire code which establishments, apartment buildings, houses, etc. has to live up to. Approved fire emergency plans, ownership of extinguishers and correctly placed working fire alarms are key to preventing fires. To ensure a better quality of fire safety the city of SF follows up on these places to check whether they violate the firecode. If they find a violation the owner is typically fined and the violation is recorded with the fire department.

So what information can we find about the relationship between violations and incidents in terms of occurences, types and time of day? To answer this we have created the map below. It contains all districts of SF and the violations and incidents in those districts using data from datasf.org.

It seems pretty clear that the majority of fires and violations happen in the northeastern parts of San Francisco. It would also seem that a lot of the fires in these areas are related to cooking fires and outdoor fires.

Actually out of the ~45,000 fires, roughly 30,000, or two thirds, are related to outdoor or cooking fires. The outdoor type may be too broad to interpret in relation to which violations could lead to those types of fires – or if there even is a violation that could lead to such a fire.
However it seems that there is a connection between cooking fires and outdoor fires - whenever one of the two is the most occurring fire type the other one is typically the second most occuring.

The history of fires in SF

The end of the month of October is Halloween in the US and is celebrated across the country. During this holiday we can find spikes in the number of fire incidents, especially in the years 2013, 2014 and 2016 where we can tell that there is considerably more fires than in the months preceding October.
The National Fire Protection Association (NFPA) reports of an average of ~860 home structure fires per year from 2009-2013 across the US, as well as 40+ civilian injuries and millions in property damage1.
Something quite ironic about this stat is that October is the official Fire Prevention Month in the US2.

The 4th of July is also widely celebrated in the US. The day celebrates the independence of the United States from the british, and is usually celebrated with a lot of fireworks.
Again, using some zoom and looking at the chart we can clearly see that on the 4th of July every year there is a peak in the number of fires with the biggest peak in 2017.

Looking at the line chart below one particular time of year seem to be missing some fires – christmas. Every year from the start of the holiday season until a week after christmas eve, there are no datapoints. This is a bit of a flaw in the data as there of course are fires during that period. Actually, according to the NFPA the christmas season causes an average of ~1100 building fires and dozens of civilian injuries and deaths every year across the US, because of christmas trees and christmas decoration3.

For now enough about fires.

When looking at the chart the first thing that pops out is the huge spikes in number of violations, which has some interesting patterns. The first pattern is the spikes happening about every 5 years around christmas/new years, marked with circles on the chart. These spikes occur because of the policy enforced by the San Francisco Fire Department (SFFD), whom conducts inspections on weekly, monthly, annual and semi-annual basis depending on the inspection type. Every fifth year there are certificates permits and equipment certification that expires4 and this is why there are spikes like these.

The second pattern we can observe is quite recent – mid 2016 to now. In 2015 a new version of the International Fire Code (IFC) was released with significant changes and in July 2016 the state of California updated their Fire Code with adoptions from IFC 2015. This brought major changes in standards regarding number of exits, doors, gates and turnstiles, sprinkler systems, fire alarms, etc.5
